Twelve Scales, Three Shapes

You already own the white-key shape. Here are the other two — and a drill that points your effort at the five keys that are actually new.

The reframe: don't learn 12 scales

Memorizing twelve separate finger sequences is a working-memory tax you don't need to pay. Every major scale belongs to one of three fingering shapes. Learn the three shapes and the logic that generates them, and the twelve scales fall out. You proved Shape 1 is solid by playing C and G hands-together at 120 — so two-thirds of the work left is really just five black-key scales sharing one idea.

Why this serves the mission: the keys you can't currently sight-read cold — the four-and-five-flat keys a film cue or a ballad sits in — are exactly the black-key scales below. Once your hand knows that terrain, a run in D♭ stops being a wall.

Shape 3 — black-key tonics the real work

These five are where your daily drill should live. They share one governing idea:

The rule that generates all five: the thumb never plays a black key. So you start on a long finger (2 or 3) and the thumb waits to drop on the first white key it can reach. In the left hand, all five begin with the 1 → 4 pivot (thumb on a white key, then 4 reaches over).

Heads-up on convention: for the black-key keys, method books disagree about the very first finger (some start B♭ RH on 4, not 2). The shape — start long, thumb on white keys, LH 1→4 pivot — is universal; the exact start finger is a coin-flip. Pick the version on this page, or whatever your old method book used, and stay consistent. If you learned different fingerings and want to reconcile them, ask me. This week's win: all five Shape-3 scales played hands-separately, one octave up and down, from memory, cleanly. Hands-together on whichever two feel closest (B♭ and E♭ are usually first). Don't chase 120 here — chase zero errors at whatever tempo delivers it.

Check the logic

1. B major and F major each differ from the home shape by changing:

Each is a one-hand variant: B keeps the home RH (LH starts on 4); F keeps the home LH (RH adds a 4 on B♭). That's what makes them cheap to learn.

2. Why do all five black-key scales start on finger 2 or 3, never the thumb?

The governing rule: the thumb never plays a black key in a scale. A black-key tonic forces you to start on a longer finger and let the thumb drop on the first white key.

3. The left hand in B♭, E♭, A♭, and D♭ major all share which pivot?

All four LH fingerings run 3 2 1 4 3 2 1 — the thumb (1) lands on a white key, then finger 4 reaches over. Learn that pivot once and it covers four keys.

4. Given the home shape, which scale needs the LEAST new practice?

D major is Shape 1 — the identical fingering to C and G, which you already play at tempo. It's transfer, not new learning.

5. The target for the black-key scales this week is best described as:

Accuracy first, always. Speed is a free byproduct of correct reps; a wrong note drilled fast is a wrong note you'll have to un-learn. Let tempo follow cleanliness.

This week at the piano — the rotation

Same ~8-minute opening block, now weighted toward Shape 3. The principle is interleaving: rotating between scales rather than blocking one to death. It feels harder in the moment and produces markedly better retention — the research on random vs. blocked practice is unambiguous (Carter, Bulletproof Musician; Gebrian on interleaving).

  1. Warm transfer (1 min). One Shape-1 scale (rotate C→G→D→A→E across days), hands together, to wake the hand up. This is review, not work.
  2. The new five, interleaved (5–6 min). Each day pick two Shape-3 scales and alternate them: B♭ right hand, E♭ right hand, B♭ left hand, E♭ left hand, then a hands-together attempt on each. Next day shift the pair (E♭+A♭, then A♭+D♭, then D♭+G♭, then G♭+B♭…). Every Shape-3 scale gets touched every 2–3 days.
  3. One Shape-2 (1 min). B or F major, alternating days, to keep them alive.
  4. Honest finish (1 min). One slow, deliberately perfect pass of the hardest scale you touched. End on a clean rep.
The single rule that matters most: never play faster than your error-free speed. When a slip happens, stop, find the exact note, and play just that crossing slowly three times before continuing. That targeted error-correction — not volume of repetition — is what separated the fastest learners in the UT Austin practice study.
