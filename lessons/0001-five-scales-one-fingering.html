Five Scales, One Fingering

C, G, D, A, and E major all use the exact same fingers. Learn one pattern, get five scales — in the five keys your pop and film repertoire lives in.

Why this is lesson one

Your mission is to learn pieces faster. The single biggest reason scale work serves that mission: a scale is the map of a key under your fingers. When your hands already know where the notes of D major sit, a run in a film arrangement stops being eight decisions and becomes one. C, G, D, A, and E are guitar-friendly keys, which makes them disproportionately common in pop and rock — so this one pattern pays rent immediately.

The pattern

Fingering (ascending)How it feels
Right hand1 2 3 1 2 3 4 5Three fingers, thumb tucks under, four fingers to the top.
Left hand5 4 3 2 1 3 2 1Four fingers, then finger 3 crosses over the thumb.

Descending is the same fingers in reverse. Three things to internalize:

  • The RH thumb tucks under immediately after finger 3. Start the tuck early — the thumb travels under the palm while 2 and 3 are still playing. (Verified fingering: LearnMusicTheory.net chart, confirmed by PianoScales.org.)
  • The hands mirror each other. LH descending feels like RH ascending. Don't fight it — use it.
  • Hands together, the third fingers always land at the same moment. In all five of these scales, both 3s strike together (twice per octave). That's your built-in checkpoint: if the 3s don't meet, something slipped.
This week's win: C major and G major, hands together, one octave up and down, from memory, at a slow steady pulse (♩ = 60). D, A, and E follow with the identical fingering — that's the point.

Try it now — fingering trainer

Pick a scale and hand, then tap the finger numbers in order (keyboard keys 1–5 work too). Do each scale until you get one clean error-free pass from memory — recalling before checking is what builds retention, not re-reading the chart.

   
Which finger plays the first note?

Check yourself

1. Right hand ascending, G major: which finger plays C (the 4th note)?

The thumb. After 1-2-3 on G-A-B, the thumb tucks under to play C: 1 2 3 1 2 3 4 5.

2. Right hand ascending, D major: which finger plays F♯?

F♯ is the 3rd note of D major (D-E-F♯), so finger 3 — right before the thumb tucks under to G.

3. Left hand ascending, A major: which finger plays D (the 4th note)?

LH ascending is 5 4 3 2 1 3 2 1: A(5) B(4) C♯(3) D(2).

4. Ascending in the right hand, the thumb tucks under immediately after which finger?

After finger 3 — the signature move of this fingering family. (At the very top the run just ends 4–5; no tuck.)

5. Which left-hand finger starts every scale in this family?

The pinky (5). LH starts at the bottom of its hand position: 5 4 3 2 1 3 2 1.

This week at the piano — 8 minutes a day

Slot this at the start of your normal 30–45 minute session. The structure matters more than the minutes — it uses retrieval practice (recall before looking) and spacing (a little daily beats a lot once), the two best-evidenced tools for making motor learning stick (Carter, Bulletproof Musician; Gebrian).

  1. Recall first (1 min). Before touching the keys, say today's fingering out loud from memory. Only check the reference chart if you blank.
  2. Two scales per day, in rotation (6 min). Day 1: C + G. Day 2: D + A. Day 3: E + C. Keep rotating. For each: right hand alone slowly until one clean pass, left hand alone the same, then — only if both hands felt easy — try hands together at half that speed, listening for the third fingers landing together.
  3. One honest rep (1 min). Finish with a single slow, deliberately perfect pass of your best scale of the day. End on accuracy, not speed. Speed comes free later; wrong notes practiced today are wrong notes you'll own forever.
Tempo rule: never faster than you can play with zero errors. The pianists who learned fastest in the UT Austin practice study weren't the ones who played the most — they were the ones who treated every error as a precise location to fix slowly, immediately (Kageyama, "8 Things Top Practicers Do Differently"). That principle is the core of this whole curriculum, and we'll apply it to your pieces next.
